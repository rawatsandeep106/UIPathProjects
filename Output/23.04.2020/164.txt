<h3>Prudent Commercial Real Estate Loan workouts</h3>

<!-- Body of the letter -------------------- -->
<div class="letter_body">

<p>The Federal Reserve, along with the other financial regulators of the Federal Financial Institutions 
	Examination Council&nbsp;(FFIEC),<a href="#Footnote1" name="Footref1" title="Footnote 1"><sup>1</sup></a> 
   has adopted the attached policy statement on <em>Prudent Commercial Real Estate Loan Workouts</em>. The 
   Federal Reserve and the other financial regulators issued this policy statement to update longstanding 
   guidance regarding the workout of CRE loans, especially in light of recent increases in such workouts. 
   This guidance is intended to promote prudent CRE loan workouts at regulated financial institutions and 
   to ensure examiners take a balanced and consistent approach in reviewing institutions’ workout activities. 
   If conducted in a reasonable and prudent manner, such workouts are often in the best interest of both the 
   institution and the borrower.</p>
   
<p>Financial institutions that implement prudent loan workout arrangements after performing comprehensive 
	reviews of borrowers’ financial conditions will not be subject to criticism for engaging in these efforts, 
   even if the restructured loans have weaknesses that result in adverse credit classifications.  In addition, 
   renewed or restructured loans to creditworthy borrowers on reasonable terms will not be subject to adverse 
   classifications solely because the value of the underlying collateral declined.</p>
   
<p>The examiner’s evaluation of a loan workout should be based upon the fundamentals of the particular loan, 
	considering the project’s current and stabilized cash flows, debt service capacity, guarantor support, 
   and other factors relevant to the borrower’s ability and willingness to repay the debt.</p>

<div class="SectionHeader">Overview of Guidance</div>

<p>The statement sets forth the appropriate standards for evaluating the management practices, workout 
	arrangements, credit classification, regulatory reporting, and accounting for prudent CRE loan workouts, 
   including:</p>

<ul style="margin-left:7ex;">
	<li class="ds">Risk management elements for the loan workout program</li>
	<li>Loan workout arrangements
		<ul>
      	<li class="ds">Analyzing the repayment capacity of the borrower</li>
      	<li class="ds">Evaluating guarantees</li>
      	<li class="ds">Assessing collateral values</li>
		</ul>
   </li>
   <li>Classification of loans
		<ul>
      	<li class="ds">Loan performance assessment for classification purposes</li>
      	<li class="ds">Classification of renewals or restructuring of maturing loans</li>
      	<li class="ds">Classification of troubled CRE loans dependent on the sale of collateral for 
         	repayment</li>
      	<li class="ds">Classification and accrual treatment of restructured loans with a partial 
         	charge-off</li>
		</ul>
   </li>
   <li>Regulatory reporting and accounting considerations
		<ul>
      	<li class="ds">Implications for interest accrual</li>
      	<li class="ds">Restructured loans</li>
      	<li class="ds">Allowance for loan and lease losses </li>
		</ul>
   </li>
</ul>

<p>The statement includes examples of CRE loan workouts.  The examples are provided for illustrative 
	purposes only, and reflect examiners’ analytical processes for credit classifications and 
   assessments of institutions’ accounting and reporting treatments for restructured loans.  While 
   the statement includes references and background materials related to regulatory reporting and 
   accounting, appraisals and valuation concepts, and classification definitions, it does not change 
   existing regulatory reporting or accounting guidance or standards.  Rather, the guidance addresses 
   supervisory expectations for reporting that reinforces longstanding guidance.</p>

<div class="SectionHeader">Scope</div>

<p>This statement applies to loans that are secured by multifamily property, and nonfarm nonresidential 
	property where the primary source of repayment is derived from rental income associated with the 
   property (that is, loans for which 50&nbsp;percent or more of the source of repayment comes from 
   third-party, nonaffiliated, rental income) or the proceeds of the sale, refinancing, or permanent 
   financing of the property.  Further, the guidance covers loans for land development and construction 
   loans (including 1&ndash; to 4&ndash;family residential and commercial construction loans), other land 
   loans, loans to real estate investment trusts (REITs), and unsecured loans to developers.  This is 
   consistent with the definition for CRE loans in SR letter&ndash;07-1, &quot;Interagency Guidance on 
   Concentrations in Commercial Real Estate.&quot;</p>

<div class="SectionHeader">Conclusion</div>

<p>Federal Reserve Banks are asked to distribute this letter to financial institutions supervised by the 
	Federal Reserve in their districts, as well as to their own supervisory and examination staff.  If you 
   have any questions, please contact Jon&ndash;Greenlee, Associate Director, Risk Management, at 
   (202)&nbsp;452-2962; Sabeth&nbsp;Siddique, Assistant Director, Credit Risk, at (202)&nbsp;452-3861; 
   Robert&nbsp;Walker, Senior Supervisory Financial Analyst, Credit Risk, at (202)&nbsp;452-3429; 
   Virginia&nbsp;Gibbs, Senior Supervisory Financial Analyst, Credit Risk, at (202)&nbsp;452-2521; 
   Lawrence&nbsp;Rufrano, Senior Supervisory Financial Analyst, Risk Policy and Guidance, at 
   (202)&nbsp;452-2808; and Linda&nbsp;Ditchkus, Senior Project Manager, Accounting Policy and 
   Disclosure, at (202)&nbsp;452-3506.</p>
